## Porter's Five Forces

### 1) Threat of New Entrants

Assess:

- capital requirements
- economies of scale
- switching costs
- brand loyalty
- regulation and distribution access
- network effects

Questions:

- How hard is market entry?
- What protects incumbents?

### 2) Supplier Power

Assess:

- supplier concentration
- substitute availability
- switching costs
- forward-integration risk

Questions:

- Can suppliers raise price or reduce quality?
- Do we have fallback options?

### 3) Buyer Power

Assess:

- customer concentration
- price sensitivity
- product differentiation
- buyer switching ease

Questions:

- Can buyers force discounts?
- Is revenue concentrated in few accounts?

### 4) Threat of Substitutes

Assess:

- alternate ways to solve problem
- price/performance tradeoff
- switching friction

Questions:

- What non-obvious substitutes exist?
- Why would buyers switch away?

### 5) Competitive Rivalry

Assess:

- competitor count
- market growth rate
- differentiation level
- exit barriers

Questions:

- Is competition price-led or value-led?
- Is the category commoditizing?

### Force Summary Table (example shape)

| Force | Intensity (1-5) | Impact | Evidence |
|---|---:|---|---|
| New Entrants | 3 | Medium | Low tooling barrier, moderate switching costs |
| Supplier Power | 2 | Low | Multiple cloud options |
| Buyer Power | 4 | High | Concentrated enterprise buyers |
| Substitutes | 3 | Medium | Manual + adjacent tools |
| Rivalry | 4 | High | Dense direct competitor set |

## Blue Ocean Strategy

Use Four Actions Framework:

- **Eliminate**: industry assumptions to remove
- **Reduce**: over-served factors to downshift
- **Raise**: under-delivered factors to exceed
- **Create**: net-new value factors

Goal: lower cost and raise buyer value simultaneously.

Execution:

1. Map current competition factors
2. Choose eliminate/reduce for cost release
3. Choose raise/create for differentiated value
4. Validate willingness to switch/pay

## Positioning Analysis

### Positioning Map

1. Select 2 customer-critical dimensions (e.g., Price vs Complexity)
2. Plot competitors and current offer
3. Identify gaps
4. Validate gap has real demand and channel accessibility

### Differentiation Lenses

1. **Product**: feature/performance/design/integrations
2. **Service**: support/onboarding/response/success
3. **Brand**: trust/community/authority/values
4. **Price & Packaging**: premium/value, transparency, plan architecture

### Positioning Statement Template

```text
For [target customer]
Who [need/opportunity]
Our product is [category]
That [key benefit]
Unlike [main alternative]
Our product [primary differentiation]
```

## Competitive Intelligence Model

### Information Sources

Public:

- websites/blogs/docs
- press/news/funding announcements
- reviews (G2/Capterra)
- social/community discussions
- job posts, patent filings, SEC filings

Direct:

- win/loss interviews
- customer interviews
- sales/support feedback
- product trials and demos

### Competitor Profile Template

For each priority competitor capture:

- company snapshot (size/funding/stage)
- product and target segment
- pricing + packaging
- GTM motion and channels
- strengths/weaknesses
- strategic direction and likely next moves

## Pricing Positioning

Segment typical positioning:

- **Premium** (top quartile): differentiated/high-touch
- **Mid-market** (middle): broad balanced value
- **Value** (bottom quartile): low-friction self-serve

Build matrix across entry/mid/enterprise tiers and infer:

- what pricing signals
- where packaging gaps exist
- where margin pressure appears

## Go-to-Market Choice Patterns

- **Direct competition**: head-to-head with clear value superiority
- **Niche focus**: narrow segment beachhead (specialist > generalist)
- **Disruptive wedge**: low-end/non-consumption entry then upmarket
- **Platform play**: ecosystem/network-effect route

### Beachhead Quality Criteria

- specific and reachable segment
- acute pain
- low/moderate entrenched competition
- willingness to pay
- credible expansion path

## Sustainable Advantage Tests

Common moat types:

- network effects
- switching costs
- scale economies
- brand trust
- proprietary tech/data
- regulatory barriers

Test each claim:

1. Copyable in <2 years?
2. Material to customer buying decision?
3. Can we execute better than peers?
4. Durable across market shifts?

If any answer is “no”, treat as weak or temporary.

## Monitoring Cadence

- **Weekly**: release notes, major announcements
- **Monthly**: win/loss review, positioning-map refresh
- **Quarterly**: full strategic review
- **Annually**: market structure reassessment

Track: product moves, pricing changes, key hires, partnerships, customer wins/losses, growth signals.
